Progress Outline and Documentation

Theory

  • What is a thermocouple?
    • A thermocouple is a simple electrical device that measures temperature. It is made by joining two wires of different metal types together at one end, and adding a connector at the other end. Due to a large variety of different metal combinations, different types of thermocouples exist, and these different types are denoted by letters from the alphabet. For example. a type T thermocouple signifies that one of the wires being joined is copper, while the other is constantan. 
  • How does a thermocouple work? 
    • A thermocouple is possible thanks to the "Seebeck effect," named after Thomas Seebeck. This effect states that "a temperature difference between two dissimilar electrical conductors or semiconductors produces a voltage difference between the two substances" (TechTarget, 2008). Otherwise stated, a change in temperature at the end where the two wires are joined will cause a change in voltage across the wires, therefore relating temperature to voltage. Fortunately, this is a direct linear relationship, encapsulated by the following equation:

V = αΔT

                    where V = voltage [V]

                               α = Seebeck coefficient [V/K]

                            ΔT = Change in temperature [K]

 

As already stated, thermocouples come in different types (Type K vs Type T, instance). Each type can have two different grades of wire, called "grade wire" and "extension grade wire' (i.e. Type K Thermocouple Grade Wire and Type K Extension Grade Wire). Grade wire refers to the wire used at the junction of the two different metals, which is the probe that will be sensing the change in temperature at a certain location. Extension grade wire refers to a thermocouple wire that is meant to span a longer distance (i.e. the sensing probe and the MCU are far apart). The extension grade wire has a lower accuracy, but it is also generally cheaper. Another consideration is whether a thermocouple wire has "special limits of error (SLE)" or "standard limits of error." Special limits of error thermocouple means that a higher grade of wire was used, therefore giving higher accuracy than the standard grade. This of course means that it is more expensive.

Practical Applications on Formula Electric 

Which type should the team buy to implement in the vehicle?

  • One of the more ideal converters to use would be the MAX31850/MAX31851 cold-junction compensated 1-wire thermocouple-to-digital converter. The main reason for it being the ideal type is that you can add an indefinite number of thermocouples on the same wire that is coming from the MCU, meaning that it saves many pins. Also, the IC comes with a unique 64-bit serial code, meaning that temperature data can be read from any connected thermocouple along the bus. Furthermore, the MAX31850 supports K, J, N, T, and E Type thermocouples whereas the MAX31851 supports Types S and R, so it would give the team flexibility regarding which type to use. Moreover, it has a 1-wire multidrop capability, which allows power to be obtained from the interface (Parasite-Powered Mode). The IC also has four dedicated address pins that can allow the user to determine the location of up to 16 thermocouples. Regarding the cost:

    $3.10 USD on www.maximintegrated.com
    $5.30 CAD on www.digikey.ca
